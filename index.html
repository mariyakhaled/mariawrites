About the Author

Between Words

Maria Khalid holds an MPhil in American Studies from the Area Study Centre, Quaid-i-Azam University Islamabad, where her research focused on South Asian American Literature, minority history, and insurgency. She believes in retrieving missing minority narratives through literature.

Professionally, she has worked as a copy editor at an open-source organization and as an ESL instructor at a university. Writing and reading have always been at the heart of everything she does, as a researcher, editor, and now as a poet.

After a long hiatus, Maria has returned to creative writing, sharing poetry, fiction, and book reviews with a growing readership. Writing in English as a Pakistani poet comes with its own challenges. Limited local publication avenues have only sharpened her resolve. She draws inspiration from a rich and eclectic range of voices, including Daud Kamal, Agha Shahid Ali, Anne Carson, Mary Oliver, and the great Urdu poet Ghalib.

Every act of writing remains a learning experience, a quiet and deliberate conversation between her words and the world. She can also be found on Medium as mariyakhaled.

How Much a Heart Can Take

In this chapbook, poetry becomes a quiet refuge for those navigating the tender terrain of longing, grief, and loss. Twenty-two emotionally resonant poems trace the slow unraveling of memory, love, and the ache that lingers in absence.

With a lyrical voice and introspective tone, each poem invites readers to sit with sorrow, celebrate vulnerability, and find beauty in emotional honesty. How Much is Lost in Translation

How much is lost in translation? A word, a phrase, a look, a sob, a shout, a heartbreak rendered meaningless in a strange flow of syllables, sounding foreign and distant, like swarming street signs in a land with its own moon, songs and tales and tunes. How much is lost in translation? The emphasis in syllables, the abrupt pause in the middle, the punctuation throwing silence like a fireball on the cruel cold outside my apartment window, where seasons and sky carry names of their own unfamiliar to my strange heart.
